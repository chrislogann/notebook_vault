**Sid Meier's Civilization 1** is a turn-based strategy game designed by Sid Meier and Bruce Shelley in 1991. The game casts you in the role of a legendary leader guiding an **entire civilization through many generations**, beginning with the founding of the world's first cities in 4000 BC and culminating in the imminent colonization of space in the modern era.

To achieve ultimate victory, you must juggle military, economic, and scientific priorities in a highly competitive global environment.

---

### **The Core Gameplay Loop**

Your responsibilities as ruler span five major areas: **economy, diplomacy, exploration, technology research, and the military**. The complexity of your choices scales as your empire expands:

- **Tactical Level (Early Game):** Your initial focus is highly tactical, requiring you to find suitable locations to found cities, direct local terrain exploration, and decide whether to produce initial military units or city improvements.
- **Strategic Level (Mid-to-Late Game):** As your empire grows, you must establish grand strategies, including negotiating war or peace with immediate neighbors, exploring and colonizing overseas, shifting your government type, and focusing your scientific pipeline.

---

### **The Pillars of Empire Management**

#### **1. City Founding and Management**

Cities are the economic and industrial powerhouses of your empire, acting as processing plants for the surrounding terrain.

- **Raw Materials:** Citizens harvest adjacent tiles to generate **Food** (to grow the population), **Resources/Shields** (to build units, improvements, or Wonders), and **Trade** (which is divided into tax revenue, science bulbs, and luxuries).
- **Population Happiness:** As cities grow, keeping the population stable is a critical priority. If unhappy citizens outnumber happy ones, the city falls into **civil disorder**, halting all production, taxes, and research. This is managed by building improvements like Temples, utilizing Specialists (Entertainers), adjusting the luxury rate, or establishing martial law.

#### **2. Technology Research**

Acquiring new knowledge is essential for your civilization's survival. The game features **over 70 technological advances** (spanning from basic Bronze Working and Pottery to high-tech Robotics and Nuclear Fission). Your civilization's research speed depends on the percentage of trade you allocate to science. Unlocking advances updates your city production options, letting you construct increasingly powerful military units and advanced city infrastructure.

#### **3. Special Units & Infrastructure**

Beyond standard military units, three specialized, non-combat units are crucial to your strategy:

- **Settlers:** Serve as pioneers who found new cities or act as engineers to build roads, irrigate land, dig mines, and clean up environmental damage.
- **Diplomats:** Serve as emissaries and spies who can establish embassies, investigate enemy cities, steal technology, perform industrial sabotage, bribe enemy units, and incite revolts to annex foreign cities.
- **Caravans:** Band of merchants used to establish lucrative trade routes between cities or carry valuable construction resources to help rush the completion of Wonders.

#### **4. Wonders of the World**

There are **21 unique Wonders of the World** (7 each for Antiquity, the Middle Ages, and the Industrial Age). Each Wonder can only be built once globally. They grant highly powerful, often continent-wide or civilization-wide bonuses (such as the Pyramids allowing government changes without anarchy, or the Hoover Dam providing clean hydroelectric power to an entire continent) and heavily boost your final score.

#### **5. Government Types**

Your empire can be ruled under **six distinct governments**, which you adopt by triggering a revolution (which causes a temporary period of Anarchy):

- **Despotism & Monarchy:** High military control and easier warfare, but restricted trade and resource output (with Monarchy being vastly superior for growth).
- **Communism:** Flat corruption rates regardless of distance, making it ideal for massive, far-flung empires.
- **The Republic & Democracy:** Drastically boost trade and eliminate corruption (under Democracy), but make large-scale military campaigns incredibly difficult because deployed units cause severe domestic unhappiness. Furthermore, a Senate reviews your foreign policy and can force you to accept peace offers.

#### **6. Environmental Caretaking**

As your empire enters the industrial age, heedless production and the use of nuclear weapons generate **pollution**. If nine or more map tiles globally remain polluted for too long, it triggers **global warming**, causing catastrophic geographic changes (such as turning fertile coastal grasslands into swamps and interior forests into deserts) and severely reducing global food and resource output.

---

### **How to Win the Game**

A game of _Civilization 1_ automatically ends when a predetermined year is reached (ranging from 2020 AD on Emperor to 2100 AD on Chieftain). To win, you must accomplish one of two goals:

1. **Conquer the World:** Physically eliminate every other rival civilization on the globe.
2. **Win the Space Race:** Be the first civilization to successfully construct and launch a colony spaceship that reaches the Alpha Centauri star system.
