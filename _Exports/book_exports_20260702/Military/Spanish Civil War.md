The Spanish Civil War (1936–1939) was a foundational conflict of the 20th century that transformed from a domestic military coup into a "world war by proxy." It pitted the democratically elected Second Republic against a Nationalist rebellion led by General Francisco Franco.

### 1. Origins and the "Cauldron of Incompatibilities"
The conflict emerged from deep-seated social and political fractures in Spain, including extreme class disparities, the struggle between centralism and regional autonomy (Catalonia and the Basque Country), and the clash between authoritarianism and libertarianism. The Republic was often described as a "cauldron of incompatibilities," where internal divisions between Communists, Anarchists (CNT-FAI), and anti-Stalinist Marxists (POUM) frequently paralyzed the war effort.

### 2. Nationalist Cohesion
In contrast to the Republic, the Nationalist forces achieved a high degree of cohesion by synthesizing right-wing, centralist, and authoritarian ideologies. General Franco consolidated his power by:
- **Commanding the Army of Africa:** His control over these elite, battle-hardened colonial troops provided the military leverage necessary to dominate the rebellion.
- **Forced Unification:** In 1937, Franco issued the Decree of Unification, merging the radical Falange and the traditionalist Carlists into a single state party (FET), effectively neutralizing internal political rivals.
- **The "Crusade" Narrative:** The Nationalist cause was framed as a "Second Reconquista," supported by the Catholic Church, which provided spiritual legitimacy to the military struggle.

### 3. Foreign Intervention
The war was internationalized by external powers who used Spain as a testing ground:
- **Axis Powers:** Nazi Germany and Fascist Italy provided critical military support, including the first major military airlift in history and the testing of *blitzkrieg* tactics (e.g., the bombing of Guernica).
- **Soviet Union:** The USSR provided weapons and advisers to the Republic, but this aid came with severe political strings, leading to the infiltration of the Republican military and the suppression of non-communist leftist factions.
- **Western "Non-Intervention":** Britain, France, and the U.S. maintained an arms embargo that crippled the Republic, while major corporations (such as Texaco, Ford, and GM) actively supplied Franco on credit, proving decisive to the Nationalist victory.

### 4. The "Fifth Column" and Internal Repression
The war was marked by extreme paranoia and institutionalized violence on both sides:
- **The Fifth Column:** The term originated from General Emilio Mola’s claim that Nationalist sympathizers were hidden within Republican-held Madrid. This narrative fueled "spy mania" and justified the suppression of political rivals within the Republic.
- **Terror:** Both sides engaged in systematic extrajudicial killings—the "Red Terror" in Republican zones and the "White Terror" in Nationalist areas—as a means of ideological cleansing.

### 5. Military Trajectory
The war was defined by the Republic's inability to maintain a unified military strategy. While the Republic initially relied on spontaneous, decentralized militias, it struggled to transition to a professional "People's Army." By 1938, Nationalist forces utilized superior coordination and German-tested tactics to split the Republican territory in two. The war effectively ended in early 1939 when Barcelona fell to Nationalist forces with almost no resistance, signaling the total collapse of Republican morale and military capacity.
