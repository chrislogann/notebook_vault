The Hungarian Revolution of 1956 was a spontaneous, nationwide uprising against the Soviet-backed Communist regime. Based on the provided notes, the revolution can be summarized through the following key dynamics:

### 1. The Catalyst: Oppression and "Salami Tactics"
The revolution was a reaction to years of brutal rule under Mátyás Rákosi. The regime had employed "salami tactics"—a strategy of slicing away democratic opposition and nationalizing the economy piece by piece—to consolidate absolute power. This was accompanied by colonial-style exploitation, most notably the secret, lopsided extraction of Hungary’s uranium reserves by the Soviet Union under the guise of a joint venture codenamed "Bauxite."

### 2. The Fury of the Streets: Vigilantism
A defining feature of the uprising was the emergence of spontaneous, violent vigilantism directed at the State Security Authority (AVO), the regime's secret police.
- **Instant Justice:** Because the AVO was viewed as the primary instrument of state terror, enraged citizens bypassed formal legal processes to deliver "popular judgments."
- **Brutality:** Retaliation was often triggered by specific atrocities, such as AVO officers firing into peaceful crowds. In events like the siege of the Communist Party headquarters at Republic Square, AVO officers were massacred by mobs.
- **Symbolic Code:** Despite the frenzied violence, the mobs adhered to a symbolic code of conduct; they frequently left the victims' cash untouched on the ground, signaling that it was "blood money" and that the uprising was not about theft.

### 3. The "Cauldron of Incompatibilities"
The revolution highlighted the deep internal fractures within the Hungarian state. The leadership of the revolution, including associates of Prime Minister Imre Nagy, struggled to maintain control over the street-level violence. They strongly condemned the lynchings, fearing that the brutality would defile the purity of their cause and provide the Soviet Union with a pretext for intervention.

### 4. The Aftermath
The revolution resulted in the lynching of an estimated 90 to 100 AVO officers. While the uprising represented a desperate attempt to reclaim national sovereignty and end Soviet colonial extraction, the pent-up fury of the population and the resulting chaos created a volatile environment that the revolutionary leaders found difficult to manage or contain.
