The Russian Revolution (1917–1921) was a transformative collapse of the Tsarist order, driven by the intersection of systemic economic exploitation, the pressures of World War I, and the subsequent rise of a totalizing Bolshevik state. Based on your vault notes, the revolution can be summarized through these key thematic pillars:

### 1. The Economic Roots: "Urban Serfdom"
Before the revolution, the Russian state functioned as a guarantor of private industrial interests. In centers like St. Petersburg, a massive labor surplus allowed factory owners to treat workers as expendable, a system described as **"Urban Serfdom."** The state police consistently sided with ownership in labor disputes, denying workers institutional outlets for grievances. This lack of safety valves made radical political upheaval inevitable once structural tensions were ignited by the external catalyst of World War I.

### 2. The Catalyst: War and the Erosion of Legitimacy
World War I acted as a "catastrophic magnifying glass" that exposed the corruption of the Russian political economy. The war effort was framed by revolutionaries as a "capitalist" venture, fueled by:
- **Disparity of Sacrifice:** The contrast between the luxury of the elite and the catastrophic casualties of the masses.
- **Procurement Corruption:** Widespread anger over lucrative government contracts awarded to private suppliers.
- **Delegitimization:** By framing commercial greed as a state-sanctioned crime, revolutionaries successfully stripped the Tsarist regime of its moral authority.

### 3. The Reactionary Pivot and "Dual Power"
Following the February Revolution, the liberal-centrist elite (the Kadet Party) attempted to protect private property by aligning with the military brass, most notably during the **Kornilov Affair**. This failed attempt at "economic militarization"—using martial law to protect assets—demonstrated the elite's inability to restore order. The subsequent "Dual Power" struggle between the Provisional Government and the Petrograd Soviet created a vertical conflict that ultimately allowed the Bolsheviks to seize control.

### 4. Financial Warfare and Forced Nationalization
In the wake of the October coup, the financial sector became the "last line of defense" for the old order. Private banks and the State Treasury initiated a **"Banking Blockade,"** refusing to issue funds to the new government. The Bolsheviks responded with:
- **Armed Occupation:** Deploying Red Guards to physically seize bank premises.
- **Forced Nationalization:** Declaring the state takeover of all banks as a tactical necessity to break the institutional strike.
- **Currency Collapse:** The divorce between political power and financial stability led to a rapid devaluation of the rouble, rendering traditional financial resistance obsolete.

### 5. The Revolutionary Backlash: "Class Genocide"
The final stage of the revolution utilized dehumanizing rhetoric to mobilize the masses for the total eradication of the business class. Lenin’s use of biological metaphors—labeling bankers and factory owners as **"lice," "fleas," and "parasites"**—provided a moral mandate for:
- **Expropriation:** The violent reclamation of "stolen" wealth, ranging from the burning of manor houses in the countryside to the systematic execution of property owners by the Cheka.
- **Institutionalized Terror:** The Cheka transitioned from a temporary security measure to a permanent organ of state control, enforcing grain requisitions and ideological conformity.

### 6. Comparative Context
Your notes highlight that the Russian experience serves as a historical blueprint for the **"mechanisms of state collapse"** and **"ideological terror."** Unlike the Spanish Civil War, which was a "cauldron of incompatibilities" between regionalists and centralists, the Russian Revolution was a vertical conflict that replaced the old symbiosis of industry and empire with an absolute authority that viewed the existence of the business class as a parasitic threat to be eradicated.
