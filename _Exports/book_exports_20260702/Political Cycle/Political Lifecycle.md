### Overview

The lifecycle of emerging democratic governments frequently demonstrates a predictable trajectory from grassroots idealism to institutional corruption and, ultimately, administrative collapse. This cycle reveals how the noble intentions of early constitutional movements are continuously threatened by the realities of political power and human ambition.

### Phase 1: Grassroots Idealism and Fundamental Law

New governments typically emerge from a collective desire to escape prior administrative corruption or political disenfranchisement. To ensure the new government reflects the will of the people, citizens intentionally bypass experienced politicians in favor of "common farmers" and representatives of the "great middle class." These founding architects are selected specifically because they appear to have **"no personal ambition to gratify, no animosities to resent, no friends to favor."** During this initial phase, political energy is effectively channeled into community change, successfully establishing fundamental laws and securing basic human rights.

### Phase 2: The Transition to Power and Factional Drama

As soon as the machinery of the new state is set in motion, idealism is rapidly displaced by tribalism and the temptation of prestige. The sudden availability of government patronage, federal resources, and national political influence quickly swallows the new representatives in intense factional drama. The same ordinary citizens who were elected to humbly serve the public rapidly transform into self-interested "Captains, Colonels, Generals, and Governors." The daily business of constructive governance is sidelined, and legislative bodies become completely distracted by the spoils of office and the pursuit of higher political appointments.

### Phase 3: Systemic Corruption and Political Purging

As factions solidify, political survival supersedes the public good. Rival leaders engage in bitter feuds to control state and military patronage. Government offices are weaponized; political purging, strategic gerrymandering, and targeted impeachments become standard tactics used to paralyze or eliminate political opponents.

- **Historical Precedent:** In the Spanish Second Republic, this phase saw the radicalization of the political climate, where the need for military discipline and foreign aid led to the centralization of power and the systematic elimination of ideological rivals through "Red" and "White" terror.
- **Tactical Weaponization:** As seen in cases like the [Kansas Impeachments](obsidian://open?file=notebook_vault%2F_Notes%2FBirth%20of%20Kansas%2FKansas%20Impeachments.md), the use of impeachment as a political tool—rather than a constitutional remedy for high crimes—serves to destabilize the executive branch, effectively turning the legislative process into a weapon of partisan warfare.

### Phase 4: Administrative Collapse and Citizen Endangerment

The ultimate consequence of this political tribalism is the complete paralysis of the state. Because the government is hopelessly divided by hostile factions, it becomes incapable of passing essential appropriations or maintaining basic order.

- **The Result:** While politicians are consumed by partisan warfare and corrupt maneuvers, the state is left powerless to perform its most fundamental duty: protecting its citizens.
- **The Spanish Lesson:** The Republic’s internal divisions and lack of military cohesion left it vulnerable to external exploitation, turning the nation into a proxy battleground for international powers. Peaceful populations are left vulnerable to lawlessness, violence, and marauding threats, forcing citizens into desperate survival measures while their elected representatives fight for prestige.

### Conclusion

This high-level cycle demonstrates that without the continuous maintenance of political trust and a vigilant check on personal ambition, the effectiveness of any new government will inevitably deteriorate into factional instability and institutional collapse. The Spanish Civil War serves as a tragic validation of this model, proving that when a state is hollowed out by internal tribalism, it loses the capacity to defend its own existence.
