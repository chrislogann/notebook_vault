The marginalization of the Falangist "Old Shirts" in Spain and the political purging associated with General James H. Lane in Kansas share a common structural dynamic: the **subordination of revolutionary or grassroots idealism to the consolidation of absolute power by a centralizing authority.**

In both cases, the initial movement—which relied on radical, populist, or "common" energy—was systematically dismantled or co-opted by a leader who viewed that original energy as a threat to their own control.

### 1. The Displacement of Revolutionary Idealism

- **Falangist "Old Shirts":** The Falange began as a radical, anti-capitalist, and proletarian-leaning movement. The "Old Shirts" sought a "National-Sindicalist" revolution that included the redistribution of wealth. However, as the Nationalist movement grew, these radicals were swamped by "modern reactionaries" and opportunists. Franco viewed the Falange’s independent revolutionary spirit as a source of instability that threatened the traditionalist "trinity" of the Army, Church, and Monarchy.
- **Kansas Grassroots:** The Wyandotte Convention was initially driven by "common farmers" and middle-class citizens who sought to escape the corruption of territorial politics. They were elected specifically because they lacked personal ambition. However, once the state government was established, this idealism was rapidly displaced by "intense factional drama" and the pursuit of prestige, as these same citizens transformed into self-interested "Captains, Colonels, and Governors."

### 2. The Mechanism of Purging and Neutralization

- **Franco’s Strategic Neutralization:** Franco utilized the "leadership vacuum" following the death of José Antonio Primo de Rivera to assert control. He used the military superiority of the Army of Africa to intimidate the Falange and eventually forced a merger (the Decree of Unification) that stripped the party of its autonomy. He sidelined leaders like Manuel Hedilla, effectively turning the Falange into a bureaucratic tool for his own regime.
- **Lane’s Purging and Patronage:** In Kansas, the political environment was defined by a "bitter feud" between Governor Robinson and General Lane over military patronage. Lane’s influence was part of a broader cycle where political survival superseded the public good. The legislature became obsessed with the "spoils of office," and political purging—including targeted impeachments and the weaponization of government offices—became the standard tactic to eliminate rivals and consolidate control over state and military appointments.

### 3. The Consequence: Administrative Paralysis and Citizen Vulnerability

- **The "Cauldron of Incompatibilities":** In both scenarios, the internal struggle for dominance created a "cauldron of incompatibilities." In Spain, this internal friction was managed by Franco through the creation of a monolithic state, effectively ending the "Old Shirts'" hope for social revolution.
- **The Powerless State:** In Kansas, the obsession with factional warfare and the purging of political opponents meant that the state became "hopelessly divided." Because the factions were so hostile, they refused to appropriate funds for the state militia. Consequently, while the politicians were busy fighting for prestige and military commissions, the state was left "entirely powerless" to protect its citizens from marauding bands and border violence.

In both instances, the "revolutionary" or "grassroots" element was sacrificed to the **"spinal column" of the state** (the military in Spain, the factional political machine in Kansas), proving that when political movements prioritize the consolidation of power over the public good, the original vision of the movement is inevitably the first casualty.
