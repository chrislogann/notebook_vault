# The Sword of the Extremes: A Comparative Analysis of Military Action and Radical Empowerment in Russia (1917) and Spain (1936)

## 1. Introduction: The Structural Instability of the Political Centre

The collapses of the Russian Provisional Government in 1917 and the Spanish Second Republic in 1936 were not merely sudden catastrophes but the terminal stages of a "hollowed centre." In Petrograd, the "Dual Power" system—a precarious arrangement between the Provisional Government and the Petrograd Soviet—created a "cauldron of incompatibilities" that rendered centrist governance a strategic impossibility. Similarly, the Spanish Republic labored under a "Fatal Paradox," attempting to compress a century’s worth of social, agrarian, and clerical reforms into a few frantic years. In both cases, the moderate vanguard—specifically the Russian Cadet (K-D) party and the Spanish middle-class liberals—was stripped of its functionality.

This instability fostered a "Manichaean environment" where ideological dogma replaced the nuanced compromise required for a liberal state. Political discourse was consumed by the conviction that "Marx has more effect than hormones," and the opponent was no longer viewed as a fellow citizen but as an internal sickness to be excised. In the streets of Petrograd and Madrid, class hatred became a visceral, reflexive force; the mere sight of the well-dressed elite provoked an annihilating resentment. As the political middle ground evaporated and "irresponsible declamation" replaced the rule of law, the stage was set for the rise of military "saviors" who intended to restore the "spinal column" of the nation through fire and steel.

## 2. The Architects of Restoration: The "Africanista" Doctrine and the Russian High Command

In both crises, the military functioned under the strategic self-perception of being the "spinal column" of the state. The officer corps viewed civilian indecision as a form of national spinelessness, necessitating a "Nationalist" restoration to halt a perceived slide into anarchy. This belief was rooted in a distinct military culture that prioritized a Castilian-style centralized authoritarianism over emerging revolutionary realities.

A striking parallel exists between General Kornilov’s _Stavka_ and the Spanish military "Directorate" composed of Generals Mola, Sanjurjo, and Francisco Franco. Both groups were influenced by an "africanista" mystique—a psychological framework born of colonial warfare and the humiliations of the Great War. In Russia, this "Russian Africanism" manifested as a warrior elite’s contempt for the "soft-handed" politicians who had allowed military hierarchy to be dismantled by _Order No. 1_. In Spain, the conspiracy was institutionalized via the UME (Unión Militar Española) and fueled by the "strategic fuel" of financier Juan March, whose wealth funded the "Dragon Rapide" mission to transport Franco to Morocco. These officers, steeped in the "Legion’s cult of slaughter," viewed themselves as a necessary "Nationalist spine" for a nation betrayed by its own government.

### Comparative Framework: Military Creed vs. Revolutionary Reality

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Attribute|Traditionalist Military Creed|Revolutionary Reality|
|**Philosophy of Rule**|Castilian-style authoritarianism; a rigid, centralized state.|The "libertarian instinct"; a focus on "freely associating co-operative communities."|
|**National Goal**|Maintenance of the "spine of the state" and absolute national unity.|The "spontaneous creativity of the masses" (Bakunin).|
|**Social Structure**|Hierarchical; a warrior aristocracy exercising total state control.|Anti-centralist; federated co-operative communities based on mutual aid.|
|**Perception of Order**|Imposed from above through strict military discipline.|Organized from below through workers' councils and regional autonomy.|
|**Core Values**|The "Code of the Hidalgo"; a contempt for money and a focus on endurance.|Proletarian solidarity; the prioritization of collective survival over hierarchy.|

This fundamental clash of cultures was the powder keg that the political intrigue of Boris Savinkov would eventually ignite in Russia, mirroring the terminal fracture that would soon split the Spanish Republic.

## 3. The Catalyst: Fractured Communications and the Rhetoric of Annihilation

The transition from political friction to military conflict was accelerated by misinformation and a "rhetoric of annihilation." In Russia, the "Emissary’s Folly" involving V.N. Lvov saw imprecise communications turn a potential alliance into a terminal fracture; Kerensky feared a Napoleonic coup, while Kornilov believed he had been invited to assume dictatorial powers. In Spain, the assassination of right-wing leader Calvo Sotelo acted as the decisive catalyst, providing the rebels with a retrospective justification for a coup they had already planned, framing it as a response to a total collapse of public order.

Tactically, the initial military moves revealed the gap between "professional rigor" and the new reality of mass mobilization. In Morocco, the rising began prematurely after being discovered by General Romerales—a figure who personified metropolitan decay as "the easiest to trick" of the generals. Meanwhile, on the mainland, General Queipo de Llano executed a masterful "bluff" in Seville. Using _Radio Seville_ as a force multiplier, he projected an image of overwhelming strength that paralyzed his opponents. Conversely, General Krymov’s III Cavalry Corps attempted a "brute force" advance on Petrograd, relying on traditional troop movements that were soon neutralized by a more modern, decentralized resistance. These initial tactical frictions led directly to the desperate, state-led arming of the radical left.

## 4. The Faustian Pact: Rearming the Radical Left

Faced with imminent military collapse, both Kerensky’s Provisional Government and José Giral’s Republican government entered into a "Faustian pact" with the radical left, effectively signing their own death warrants. In Russia, Kerensky authorized the rearming of the Bolshevik Red Guards and the release of radical prisoners, including Leon Trotsky, abdicating state legitimacy to the Petrograd Soviet.

In Spain, Prime Minister Giral followed a parallel path of institutional suicide. By decree, he dissolved the regular army—the state's primary instrument of force—and ordered the distribution of 60,000 rifles to the CNT and UGT militias. This mobilization was plagued by a catastrophic logistical failure: only 5,000 of these rifles actually possessed bolts, leaving the remainder useless until the masses stormed the Montaña Barracks to seize them by force. By handing the "victorious sword" to revolutionary militias to halt a right-wing coup, these centrist governments destroyed the state's monopoly on force, ensuring that they could never reclaim the authority they had so desperately surrendered.

## 5. Logistical Paralysis and the "Spontaneous Creativity of the Masses"

The failure of professional military rigor against "revolutionary gymnastics" was ultimately a matter of logistics and infrastructure control. While the military sought to move through traditional channels, the "libertarian instinct" turned the geography of the nation against the coup-plotters.

- **The Logistics of Proletarian Resistance (Russia):** The advance of Krymov’s III Cavalry Corps was halted not by a rival army, but by railway workers. By misdirecting troop trains, sabotaging transport networks, and severing communications, the workers created a logistical paralysis that the centralized military command was structurally incapable of overcoming.
- **The Telegraphist’s War (Spain):** In the Spanish fleet, telegraphist Benjamín Balboa intercepted Franco’s signals and warned lower-deck ratings that their aristocratic officers were "a gang of fascists." This triggered a violent naval mutiny on ships like the _Jaime I_. Crews overpowered their wardrooms and, in a chilling display of the new Manichaean reality, asked the Ministry for instructions on the **bodies of their officers**; they were told to "lower them overboard with respectful solemnity."

### Why "Revolutionary Gymnastics" Defeated "Professional Rigor"

1. **Decentralized Sabotage:** Professional militaries rely on functioning infrastructure; workers' councils (like the _Alianza Obrera_) proved that controlling the tracks and telegraph lines is as vital as controlling the barracks.
2. **The Information Gap:** The military consistently underestimated the organization of lower-level technicians and ratings, allowing the "rhetoric of threat" to be turned against the officer corps by figures like Balboa.
3. **Spontaneous Mobilization:** The ability of militias to act decisively in the first 48 hours—whether storming the Montaña Barracks or misdirecting trains—overwhelmed the slow, synchronized rising planned by the "Directorates."

## 6. The Victorious Sword: Long-Term Consequences of the Hollowing of the Centre

The ultimate consequence of these failed coups was the permanent transition of power from formal state structures to ideological militias. In Russia, the "Bykhov Captivity"—the imprisonment of Kornilov and his generals—marked the final extinguishing of military-civilian trust. General Alekseev’s resignation was a moment of profound "bad faith"; the officer corps viewed the arrests as a betrayal of the nation’s "Nationalist spine." Consequently, when the Bolsheviks moved to seize power in October, the embittered and sidelined military stood aside, refusing to defend a "Kerensky regime" that had imprisoned its own high command.

In Spain, the failure of the initial 48-hour coup led to a territorial partition and the onset of an "International Civil War." The initial naval mutiny had temporarily trapped the Army of Africa, but this was resolved by the "Axis Bridge"—the first major airlift in history, using German Junkers 52s and Heinkels to bypass the Republican fleet. This logistical shift converted a failed coup into a long war of total elimination. On one side, the "White Terror" (the military's systematic _limpieza_ or cleansing) was blessed by the Church as a way to purge "gangrenous" elements; on the other, the "Red Terror" erupted as a spontaneous, retaliatory explosion.

### The Three Core Fatalities of the Hollowing Process

1. **The Polarization Axis:** The "Reds vs. Whites" conflict destroyed the possibility of citizenship, replacing the social contract with a "rhetoric of annihilation."
2. **The Logistical Shift:** Power transitioned permanently from the state to workers' councils and revolutionary militias, creating a "cauldron of incompatibilities."
3. **The Legitimacy Void:** Centrist administrations—having hollowed out parties like the K-D—became mere "regimes" in the pejorative sense: bridges to revolution that lost the support of the military without ever gaining the loyalty of the radicals.

The historical lesson remains stark: the "Victorious Sword" of state power, once transferred to radical factions in a moment of existential desperation, can never be reclaimed by the democratic centre. When the middle ground is sacrificed to the "politics of fear," the eventual victor will not be a reformer, but the most militant extreme.
