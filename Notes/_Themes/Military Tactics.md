# THE DOCTRINE OF THE DUEL: A STRATEGIC ARCHITECTURE OF JOINT MILITARY TACTICS

## 1. The Theoretical Nexus: Distinguishing War from Warfare

In the professional application of military power, the Joint Force Commander (JFC) must first distinguish the political essence of war from the mechanical execution of warfare. War is fundamentally "socially sanctioned violence to achieve a political purpose," arising when diplomatic statecraft fails to resolve disputes. Warfare, conversely, is the "mechanism, method, or modality" of that armed conflict—it is the "how" of waging war. Joint doctrine is authoritative and must be followed except when exceptional circumstances dictate otherwise; therefore, understanding this distinction is the prerequisite for framing the operational environment and determining the necessary force structure and rules of engagement.

The foundational theories of war are rooted in the synthesis of two primary strategic perspectives:

- **Carl von Clausewitz:** Defines war as a "duel on a larger scale" and a "violent clash of wills." He characterizes war as an act of force intended to compel an enemy to fulfill our will, serving as a continuation of politics by other means.
- **Sun Tzu:** Regards war as a "matter vital to the state" and the "road to survival or ruin." He emphasizes attacking the enemy’s strategy and evaluates conflict through the five factors of moral influence, weather, terrain, command, and doctrine.

To navigate the "fog of war," the JFC must manage the shifting interplay of the "Social Trinity." This trinity consists of **emotion** (the irrational, connected to the people), **chance** and uncertainty (the non-rational, connected to the military), and **reason** (the rational, connected to the government). These non-rational variables create a volatile environment that demands commander responsiveness and versatility. The JFC leverages these attributes to seize opportunities and mitigate vulnerabilities within a violent struggle between independent, hostile wills.

## 2. The Operational Spectrum: Traditional vs. Irregular Methodologies

The JFC must leverage the synergistic effects of jointness to combine forms of warfare that suit national capabilities and the specific strategy of the adversary. This choice is guided by the two fundamental strategies in the use of force: **Annihilation**, which seeks the physical destruction of the enemy's military capacity to make them helpless to resist, and **Erosion**, which seeks to raise the cost of resistance higher than the enemy is willing to pay.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Feature|Traditional Warfare|Irregular Warfare (IW)|
|**Strategic Purpose**|Domination between nation-states or coalitions.|Legitimacy and influence over relevant populations.|
|**Primary Actors**|Nation-states and conventional forces.|State and non-state actors (Non-Westphalian).|
|**Victory Mechanisms**|**Annihilation**: Destruction of capacity and force defeat.|**Erosion**: Exhaustion of will and raising costs of resistance.|

The Joint Staff recognizes a "symmetric naming friction" regarding these methodologies. While "irregular" might suggest the uncommon, the U.S. military routinely conducts these operations; the term "irregular" is maintained specifically to highlight the non-Westphalian context of the struggle. Within this spectrum, the JFC must account for **Resistance and Partisan Forces**. These irregular actors utilize asymmetric and indirect approaches—including terrorism and disinformation—to disrupt the advantages of superior conventional militaries. Consequently, military power in IW often serves only to set the conditions for a broader "whole-of-nation" political victory.

## 3. The Hierarchy of Execution: Strategic, Operational, and Tactical Integration

The "three levels of warfare" serve as the essential cognitive framework for linking individual tactical actions to national political objectives.

1. **Strategic Level:**
    - **Principal Actors:** The President, Secretary of Defense (SecDef), and the National Security Council (NSC).
    - **Responsibilities:** Developing national policy and strategic military objectives (the "Ends"). They determine national guidance and allocate resources across the global theater.
2. **Operational Level:**
    - **Principal Actors:** Combatant Commanders (CCDRs) and JFCs.
    - **Responsibilities:** Utilizing "Operational Art" to sequence tactical actions (the "Ways"). They link strategy to tactics by designing campaigns that integrate ends, ways, and means.
3. **Tactical Level:**
    - **Principal Actors:** Tactical Units and Joint Task Forces (JTFs).
    - **Responsibilities:** The ordered arrangement and maneuver of combat elements (the "Means"). This level is defined by **Small-Unit and Crew Actions**, battles, and engagements.

The JFC must remain cognizant of the "Overlap of Levels" caused by 24-hour media and pervasive internet access. In the modern environment, a single tactical action by a lone Service member can have immediate, severe strategic implications. This necessitates a distinction between an **Operation** (a sequence of tactical actions with a common purpose) and a **Campaign** (a series of related major operations). Planning a campaign is a doctrinal requirement when the scope, time, or resource requirements of a mission exceed the capacity of a single strike, providing the framework for sustained progress toward strategic end states.

## 4. The Engine of Combat Power: Evaluating the Seven Joint Functions

Jointness implies a cross-Service combination where the sum of the Joint Force is synergistic—greater than the capability of its individual components. The JFC generates combat power by integrating seven joint functions.

- **Command and Control (C2):** The exercise of authority and direction; the art of motivating people and the science of managing forces.
- **Intelligence:** Enables understanding of the operational environment and adversary vulnerabilities to support decision-making.
- **Fires:** The use of weapon systems to create lethal or nonlethal effects.
- **Movement and Maneuver:** Disposing joint forces to secure positional advantages over the adversary.
- **Protection:** Conserving the force's fighting potential through active/passive defense and emergency management.
- **Sustainment:** The provision of logistics and personnel services to prolong operations.
- **Information:** Leverages the pervasive nature of information to influence actor perceptions and behavior while supporting human and automated decision-making.

|---|---|

|**Intelligence**|Loss of situational awareness; inability to identify opportunities.|
|**Protection**|Unnecessary depletion of combat power; loss of reserves.|
|**Sustainment**|Reduced **operational reach** and **endurance**; inability to conduct decisive operations.|
|**Information**|Loss of credibility (the **"say-do" gap**); failure to bridge the gap between military action and political perception.|

## 5. The Architecture of Unified Action: Regional and Global Synchronization

Unified Action is the synchronization of military operations with interorganizational partners to achieve **Unity of Effort**. To demonstrate the practical application of this architecture, consider the following theoretical deployments of Joint Doctrine:

- **Geographic AOR Management (Spain and Russia):** A Geographic Combatant Commander (GCC) for the European theater would manage the Area of Responsibility (AOR) containing Spain and Russia. Under the Unified Command Plan (UCP), the GCC is the vital link between national policy and the forces conducting operations. If an adversary such as Russia were to conduct transregional activities, the **Global Synchronizer** would align and harmonize plans between echelons to facilitate decentralized execution.
- **Multinational Integration (Hungary):** In operations involving Hungary as a NATO partner, the JFC must prioritize **Multinational Unity of Effort**. This requires adherence to the tenets of **Respect, Rapport, Knowledge of Partners, and Patience**. Success depends on recognizing that Hungary’s national honor is as critical as its combat capability and ensuring Hungarian forces are integrated into the planning process from inception.
- **Domestic vs. Federal Status (Kansas):** When integrating the National Guard in Kansas, the JFC must distinguish between **Title 32 and Title 10 USC status**. Normally, Kansas National Guard forces operate under the command of their Governor (Title 32) for domestic operations. However, once validated for federal employment and mobilized under Title 10, they are assigned to a CCDR for operational missions, bridging the gap between state-level security and national military strategy.

To achieve theater strategic objectives, military tactics must be integrated with the **Instruments of National Power (DIME)**:

- **Diplomatic:** Engaging foreign states to advance U.S. values.
- **Informational:** Leveraging communication to influence audiences and mitigate the "say-do" gap.
- **Military:** The coercive application or threat of force.
- **Economic:** Ensuring access to global markets and resources.

In summary, tactical success is "void" unless it is strictly aligned with the national military strategy. Whether employing a strategy of **Annihilation** or **Erosion**, the Joint Force must remain a values-based organization, ensuring every small-unit action serves the ultimate political end state of the nation.
