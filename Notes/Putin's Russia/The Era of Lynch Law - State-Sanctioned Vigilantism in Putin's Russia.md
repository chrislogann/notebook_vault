In Putin's Russia, vigilantism diverged sharply from the traditional understanding of spontaneous grassroots mob justice. Instead, it manifested as a **state-sanctioned system of arbitrary retaliation that effectively replaced the formal rule of law**. With the nation's courts rendered entirely subservient to political interests and law enforcement deeply corrupted, citizens were left without legal protection. As a result, **an "eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth" mentality became the defining order of both the Russian state and its people**.

**A Culture Modeled from the Kremlin** This culture of extrajudicial retribution was not an accident; it was modeled and encouraged from the absolute highest levels of government. **President Vladimir Putin himself set the ultimate example of "lynch law" by dismantling the major, transparent oil company Yukos and imprisoning its chief executive, Mikhail Khodorkovsky**. By bypassing formal legal principles to eliminate a political opponent, the Kremlin demonstrated to the rest of the country that arbitrary retaliation was a perfectly acceptable method for resolving disputes.

**Military Impunity in Chechnya** The most systematic and brutal manifestations of this state-sponsored vigilantism occurred during the second Chechen war, where **retaliation was elevated to a legitimate justification for murder**. A defining precedent was the highly politicized trial of Russian Colonel Yury Budanov, who abducted, raped, and murdered an 18-year-old Chechen girl named Elza Kungaeva in March 2000. Budanov's defense argued that he acted out of personal retribution, believing the girl was a rebel sniper. The court's willingness to entertain this defense effectively validated "customary law" over formal Russian legal codes. The Kremlin's tacit endorsement of this vigilantism **triggered a massive wave of violent "purges" and extrajudicial murders throughout Chechnya in 2002, committed by soldiers who used the collapse of the legal system as cover** to rape, murder, and disappear citizens with absolute impunity.

This culture of impunity was further cemented by the Dai Massacre in January 2002, an event often referred to as "Budanov Case II". A special operations unit of the GRU intercepted a civilian minibus near the village of Dai. **The soldiers tortured the passengers for information, murdered all six innocent civilians—including a pregnant mother and a 69-year-old headmaster—and burned their bodies**. Instead of facing justice, the perpetrators were shielded by the military leadership, who intervened to stall investigations and transfer the accused to a prison beyond the local prosecutor's reach. The military establishment actively protected these killers, evidently considering them heroes who had loyally served the motherland.

**Vigilantism in Law Enforcement and Commerce** The normalization of "lynch law" quickly bled into civilian law enforcement and the corporate sphere. Following the _Nord-Ost_ theater siege in Moscow in late 2002, police and state authorities launched a campaign of racially based state retribution against innocent Chechens living in the capital. **Operating under a vigilantism mindset, police officers routinely planted drugs, grenades, or cartridges on Chechen citizens to fabricate criminal cases**. Victims were abducted, beaten, and forced to sign false confessions by officers declaring, "You kill us and we will kill you".

Simultaneously, the collapse of the legal framework allowed powerful oligarchs and criminal syndicates to utilize vigilante-style mob violence to seize corporate assets. In 2000, figures like Pavel Fedulev deployed private armies armed with baseball bats and iron bars, backed by corrupt special police units, to violently storm and illegally take over massive industrial enterprises like the Uralkhimmash Corporation and the Kachkanar Ore Enrichment Complex.

Ultimately, vigilantism in Putin's Russia was not an anomaly but a structural pillar of society. By systematically prioritizing political interests, military solidarity, and personal vengeance over formal judicial processes, the state cemented arbitrary retaliation as the defining characteristic of modern Russian "justice".
