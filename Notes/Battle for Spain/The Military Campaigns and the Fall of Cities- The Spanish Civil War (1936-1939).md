The War in Aragón: From Republican Failure to Nationalist Blitzkrieg

The campaigns in Aragón perfectly illustrated the contrasting military capabilities and strategies of the two factions, ultimately leading to a catastrophic division of the Republican territory.

The Failed Republican Offensive (August–September 1937)

In August 1937, the Republican general staff, led by General Vicente Rojo, launched a major offensive in Aragón aiming to capture the strategic communications hub of Saragossa and distract the Nationalists from their campaign in the north. The Republic massed an overwhelming local superiority of troops, aircraft, and Soviet tanks, spearheaded by Juan Modesto's V Corps.

However, the offensive was a tactical disaster. **Instead of using their tanks and troops to bypass small pockets of resistance and push rapidly towards Saragossa, Republican commanders bogged their forces down in costly sieges of small towns like Quinto, Codó, and Belchite**. The fighting for Belchite was particularly brutal, reducing the town to a smoking ruin of smashed masonry and corpses during six days of desperate street combat.

By the time the operation ended, the Republicans had wasted thirteen days and suffered immense losses in men and vital tank reserves, only to advance a mere ten kilometers. The offensive completely failed its primary objectives: it did not capture Saragossa, nor did it relieve the pressure on the northern front.

The Nationalist Blitzkrieg (Spring 1938)

Following the grueling winter Battle of Teruel, the Republican forces were exhausted and poorly equipped. General Franco seized the opportunity to launch a massive offensive through Aragón, deploying 150,000 men, nearly 700 guns, and some 600 aircraft.

The campaign opened on March 9, 1938, with a devastating ground and air bombardment that obliterated the Republican front lines. **This offensive was characterized by highly coordinated "blitzkrieg" tactics, utilizing German tanks and the new Stuka dive-bombers to smash defenses and exploit breakthroughs**. General Yagüe's Moroccan Corps advanced 36 kilometers in a single day, while Republican troops, many of them inexperienced conscripts, broke into a panic-stricken rout.

The Nationalist advance was relentless, pausing only briefly before pushing toward the coast. **On April 15, 1938 (Good Friday), Nationalist forces reached the seaside town of Vinaroz, successfully cutting the Republican zone in two and physically separating Catalonia from the rest of Republican Spain**.

The Tale of Two Barcelonas: 1936 vs. 1939

The fate of Barcelona encapsulates the tragic trajectory of the Republic, transforming from a bastion of revolutionary defiance in 1936 to a starved, demoralized, and broken city in 1939.

The Fierce Civilian Resistance (July 1936)

When the military coup erupted in July 1936, the Nationalist conspirators considered Barcelona a certain conquest, backed by 12,000 troops. However, they critically underestimated the determination of the city's working class.

The anarcho-syndicalist CNT and other workers' organizations immediately prepared for war, stripping gun shops bare, capturing armouries, and building barricades out of paving stones across the city. **When the military marched into the streets on July 19, they were met by a ferocious popular resistance**. The workers were surprisingly joined by the Assault Guards and the Civil Guard, who helped turn the tide. The fierce fighting culminated in the capture of rebel-held buildings and the surrender of the rebel commander, General Goded. The heroic, if costly, final assault on the Atarazanas barracks cemented the city's reputation for defiant, revolutionary bravery.

Surrender Without Resistance (January 1939)

Two and a half years later, the situation had entirely reversed. By the winter of 1938-1939, Barcelona was subjected to relentless, terrorizing bombing raids by the Italian air force and the Condor Legion. **The population was on the edge of starvation, surviving on a daily ration of just 100 grams of lentils, with people collapsing from hunger in the streets**. Industrial production had plummeted to one-tenth of pre-war levels due to raw material and electricity shortages.

When the Nationalists launched their final offensive against Catalonia in late December 1938, the Republican army was severely underequipped, lacking rifles, tanks, and serviceable artillery. Any theoretical defensive lines around Barcelona proved to be a fantasy; there was not even a kilometer of trenches built to defend the city.

**Defeatism was rife, and the defiant morale of 1936 had completely evaporated**. As the Nationalist forces approached, a massive, panic-stricken exodus began, clogging the roads toward the French border with refugees and fleeing soldiers. Consequently, **on January 26, 1939, General Yagüe's Nationalist troops entered a "dead city," occupying Barcelona "without firing a shot"**.
