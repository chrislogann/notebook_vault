### 1. Introduction: The Internal Front

The Spanish Civil War was characterized by Antoine de Saint-Exupéry not merely as a conventional military conflict, but as a "sickness" where "the enemy is within" and "one fights almost against oneself." This psychological landscape was forged by an extreme polarization between "reds" and "whites," a process where ideologies stripped opponents of both their humanity and their citizenship. In this internationalized civil war, the domestic front became a theater of Manichaean propaganda that manipulated apocalyptic images of the enemy, overriding normal human instincts and turning brothers into "faceless strangers."

While the Nationalist forces remained a coherent "trinity of army, monarchy, and Church"—unifying right-wing, centralist, and authoritarian impulses—the Republic existed as a "cauldron of incompatibilities." This internal friction, exacerbated by the siege of Madrid, fueled a pervasive paranoia. As the capital became the focal point of the struggle, the fear of hidden subversion transformed the military conflict into a hunt for the internal traitor, creating a climate where political rivals were viewed with as much suspicion as the advancing columns of General Franco.

### 2. General Emilio Mola and the "Fifth Column"

The term "fifth column" originated during the Nationalist advance on Madrid in the autumn of 1936. Its creation is explicitly credited to General Emilio Mola. As four Nationalist columns converged on the capital, Mola made the tactical assertion that a "fifth column" of sympathizers was already hidden within the city, poised to rise up and assist the attackers from the rear. This assertion, while perhaps intended as a piece of psychological warfare to embolden supporters, had a devastating impact on the Republican population.

**Original Military Meaning:** A tactical assertion by General Emilio Mola referring to a clandestine group of Nationalist sympathizers located within Republican-held Madrid, intended to provide internal support to the four conventional military columns advancing on the city from the exterior.

**Psychological Impact:** Beyond its tactical origins, the term became a primary driver of "spy mania." It transformed every citizen into a potential suspect, justifying internal repression and creating a climate where the fear of the hidden traitor was used to rationalize the elimination of political rivals.

### 3. The Anatomy of Spy Mania and Paranoia

Behind Republican lines, the atmosphere was thick with a "spy mania" that fed off the ghosts of past conflicts. This paranoia was not accidental; it was the result of political polarization that allowed extremes to manipulate fearful images of their enemies. The Republic’s "cauldron of incompatibilities" made it particularly susceptible to this decay, as the various factions struggled to find common ground against a unified Nationalist front.

This climate was predicated on three primary axes of conflict:

- **Class Interests:** The traditional struggle between the landless or industrial workers and the landed/industrial bourgeoisie.
- **State Centralism vs. Regional Independence:** The friction between those desiring a unified Spanish state (notably the Communists) and those seeking regional autonomy (such as the Catalans and Basques).
- **Authoritarianism vs. Freedom of the Individual:** The clash between disciplined, authoritarian structures (represented by the Communists and centralists) and the "libertarian" or anarchist ideals of the CNT-FAI.

Because the Nationalists successfully combined all three cohesive extremes (right-wing, centralist, and authoritarian), their internal front remained stable. Conversely, the Republic’s internal divisions meant that the "traitor" was often a member of a rival faction whose vision for Spain was viewed as an existential threat to the others.

### 4. Institutionalized Repression and the "Red Terror"

Repression within Republican territory evolved from spontaneous "revolutionary gymnastics" into institutionalized systems managed by political committees and secret police. While the initial wave of violence was often an uncoordinated reaction to the military rising, it was soon replaced by organized repression designed to maintain a "protective custody of the mind."

Historically, this "protective custody of the mind" had its roots in the 15th-century Catholic Church and the Inquisition, which sought to burn books and people to keep out religious and political heresy. The paranoia of the Civil War mirrored these older institutional repressions. The security forces—including the Assault Guards and the secret police—became the modern executors of this atavistic impulse. They enforced ideological conformity through widespread arrests and executions, justifying their actions as necessary measures to safeguard the Republic from the "fifth column," while simultaneously consolidating power for the centralist and authoritarian factions.

### 5. Case Study: The Paracuellos Massacre

The Paracuellos massacre stands as the peak incident of the paranoia generated by the Fifth Column narrative. In late 1936, as Nationalist forces approached Madrid, the Republican authorities began the evacuation of political prisoners to prevent them from joining the advancing "fifth column." In a moment of military crisis, the perceived threat of internal subversion led to large-scale killings as a preventative measure.

"The winner in any civil war... is bound to kill more because of the cycle of fear and hate." This cycle was inflamed by a "rhetoric of annihilation" that turned the military threat into a justification for domestic slaughter, ensuring that those in custody were eliminated before they could become an active internal front.

### 6. Political Weaponization: The Communist Narrative and Scapegoating

The term "fifth column" was weaponized with chilling efficiency by the Spanish Communist Party (PCE) and its Soviet advisers. By labeling political rivals as agents of fascism, the PCE justified the suppression of any group that resisted centralist control. While the Nationalist Right utilized a narrative of a "Judaeo-Masonic-Masonic" world conspiracy, the Communists focused their scapegoating on "provocateurs" and "Trotskyist-Fascists."

### 7. The Role of Fear in Military Strategy

Fear was a deliberate instrument of military strategy, most famously deployed by Nationalist General Queipo de Llano. His notorious radio broadcasts from Seville utilized a "rhetoric of annihilation," boasting of the violence his troops—particularly the Moroccan _regulares_ and the Foreign Legion—would inflict. This external rhetoric was designed to inflame the climate of suspicion within Republican territory, creating a reinforcing feedback loop with the internal paranoia.

The threat of annihilation bolstered the "authoritarians" (the Communists and centralists) within the Republic. They used the terror inspired by Queipo de Llano and the fear of the "fifth column" to justify the suppression of "libertarians" and the centralization of all power. They argued that a disciplined, authoritarian state was the only defense against the dual threat of the advancing columns and the traitors within.

### 8. Conclusion: The Legacy of Paranoia

The "politics of fear" and the "rhetoric of threat" effectively destroyed the center ground of Spanish politics, making compromise impossible. By framing the conflict through the lens of the "Fifth Column," both sides ensured that normal human instincts were overridden by ideological necessity. The ghosts of these propaganda battles have endured for decades, a testament to how the "sickness" of paranoia turned the Spanish Civil War into a conflict where ideologies transformed brothers into faceless strangers and where a nation, in its struggle for identity, fought almost against itself.
