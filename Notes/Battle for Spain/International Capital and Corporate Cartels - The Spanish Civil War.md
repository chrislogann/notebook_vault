During the Spanish Civil War (1936–1939), the political and military success of General Francisco Franco's Nationalists was heavily underpinned by domestic and international business networks:

- **Funding the Right:** Franco's pre-war right-wing political alliance, the National Counter-Revolutionary Front, was heavily financed by wealthy landowners, large corporations, and the Catholic Church. Multimillionaires like the tobacco smuggler Juan March personally contributed massive sums—reputedly around £15 million—to fund the Nationalist conspiracy and war effort.
- **Foreign Corporate Backing:** While the democratic powers maintained a policy of non-intervention, major American and British corporations actively boycotted the Spanish Republic, disrupted its trade with legal action, and delayed its credits. Concurrently, they supplied Franco with vital supplies. The president of the Texas Oil Company (Texaco), an admirer of the fascists, diverted five oil tankers en route to Spain to the Nationalist port of Tenerife on credit. Standard Oil of New Jersey, Ford, Studebaker, and General Motors supplied thousands of trucks, and the chemical giant DuPont provided 40,000 bombs to the Nationalists (routed through Germany to bypass the Neutrality Act). Franco’s under-secretary of foreign affairs, José Maria Doussinague, later admitted: _"Without American petroleum and American trucks and American credit, we could never have won the civil war"_.
- **Profiteering on Both Sides:** Some business interests exploited the conflict for pure financial gain. Most notoriously, Nazi Germany's Luftwaffe commander-in-chief, Hermann Göring, operated a secret arms-trafficking racket through a Greek intermediary. He sold older, inferior weapons to the Spanish Republic in exchange for hard currency, while simultaneously supplying his best weaponry to Franco. As Göring noted, Republican hard currency was just as good as Nationalist funds.
- **The Post-War Bank-State Alliance:** Following Franco's victory, the regime turned back land reforms and established an iron-clad relationship with five major Spanish banks. In exchange for their cooperation, these banks were legally protected from all competition (no new banks were allowed to open in Spain until 1962), allowing them to amass massive commercial empires. As one historian observed, _"The architectural symbol of the new Spain was not the church... but the bank"_.
