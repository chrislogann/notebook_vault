### 1. The Collapse of the Regular Army and the Rise of Militias (July 1936)

The immediate aftermath of the July 1936 rising presented a "fatal paradox" that effectively decapitated the Spanish Second Republic’s monopoly on organized violence. While the Casares Quiroga government initially collapsed due to its refusal to arm the working class, the subsequent Giral administration—formed by a personal friend of Azaña who realized the "terrible truth" of the military rebellion—attempted to preserve the state by dismantling its primary instrument of defense.

**Immediate Structural Consequences of the July Decrees:**

- **Dissolution of the Regular Army:** By dissolving the regular army by decree, the Giral government sought to neutralize rebellious garrisons but inadvertently destroyed the administrative framework for a centralized defense.
- **Delegation of Sovereignty:** Sovereignty shifted from the state to the streets as the government armed trade union organizations (CNT and UGT), transforming political parties into paramilitary recruiters.
- **The "Fatal Paradox":** To defend republican legality, the state was forced to empower revolutionary groups whose ultimate objective was the subversion of that very bourgeois state.
- **Systemic Tactical Incoherence:** The arming of the masses led to the "revolutionary reality" of the streets, where ideological fervor replaced professional military education (PME), leaving the Republic without a technical response to the specialized colonial forces.

### 2. The Era of "Revolutionary Gymnastics": Trade Union and Party Militias

The Republic’s initial survival depended upon spontaneous militias defined by "revolutionary gymnastics"—the cycle of strikes and uprisings intended to precipitate social revolution. These irregular formations were characterized by a total lack of technical integration; as Beevor notes, the "heavy grease" of stored rifles often had to be cleaned off with "party newspapers" by militants who had never handled a bolt-action weapon.

### 3. Transition to the "People’s Army" (Ejército Popular)

The metamorphosis from decentralized revolutionary militias to a formal "People’s Army" was a centripetal political project advocated by the Communist Party and Francisco Largo Caballero—the "Spanish Lenin." This shift represented a victory of military necessity over revolutionary idealism.

#### 1. Professional Discipline and PME

The transition replaced "revolutionary gymnastics" with traditional hierarchy. Proponents argued that "scientific socialism" required absolute discipline to face a professional enemy. This necessitated the reintroduction of ranks and the elimination of militia committees.

#### 2. Centralized Strategic Command

The defense was initially a "cauldron of incompatibilities." Professionalization aimed to unify disparate ideological columns under a single General Staff to manage complex fronts like the Guadarrama and the defense of Madrid, preventing the tactical fragmentation seen in the early weeks of the war.

#### 3. International Standing and Soviet Influence

To secure aid from the Soviet Union and project an image of sovereign stability to France, the Republic had to appear as a legitimate state with a professional army. The Comintern "advised" a policy of preserving the Republic as a "democratic revolution" stage, delaying the dictatorship of the proletariat to maintain international support.

### 4. Mobilization through Executive Decree: The Legal Framework of Conscription

The Republic utilized executive power to bypass the "impotence of the Spanish parliament" while exercising wartime authority. Mobilization was framed not as a state of war—a declaration the government avoided to maintain international standing—but as a defense of the legally appointed government following the February 1936 elections.

**The "Republic of Workers" Legal Status** The government utilized Article 1 of the 1931 Constitution, which declared Spain a **"democratic republic of workers of all classes."** By emphasizing its status as the **"legally appointed one,"** the government justified the re-incorporation of reservists and the drafting of specific age classes. As Beevor observes, **"Republican propaganda during the civil war always emphasized that its government was the legally appointed one,"** contrasting this with the Nationalist "fabrication" of a pre-emptive strike against a communist putsch.

**The Mechanism of the Decree** By governing through decree, the Giral and subsequent Caballero administrations exercised the powers of a state of war while arguing they were merely suppressing a military rebellion. This allowed the state to re-assert its administrative control over the citizen-body, though the enforcement of conscription remained inconsistent across regionalist strongholds.

### 5. Administrative and Organizational Hurdles

The formation of a cohesive force was crippled by a "cauldron of incompatibilities" involving three axes of conflict: class interest, state centralism versus regional independence, and authoritarianism versus the freedom of the individual.

"The Republic... represented a cauldron of incompatibilities and mutual suspicions, with centralists and authoritarians, especially the communists, opposed by regionalists and libertarians."

This structural friction manifested in specific military failures:

- **Regionalist Autonomy:** In Catalonia and the Basque country, regional governments frequently ignored centralist directives, leading to a fragmented "centrifugal" defense.
- **Ideological Friction:** In Seville and the Albaicín (Granada), the "feud between anarchists and communists" undermined the organization of effective counter-attacks, allowing Nationalist forces to consolidate control despite being outnumbered.
- **The "Reconquista" Counter-Imagery:** While the Nationalists used "Reconquista" imagery to provide a cohesive mythos of a crusade against "contemporary heathens," the Republic struggled to define a unified national identity, caught between the centralist demands of the Communists and the libertarian aspirations of the CNT.

### 6. Summary of Force Composition and Reliability

The struggle for control highlighted a systemic failure in Republican technical integration compared to the "africanista" mystique of the Nationalist regulars.

|---|---|
|Republican Force Components|Nationalist Regulars (Legion/Regulares)|
|**Assault Guards (Asaltos):** Urban paramilitary force; generally loyal and provided the only professional barrier in the early city fighting.|**The Foreign Legion:** A professional elite indoctrinated with a "cult of death" (Viva la Muerte) and specialized in "ruthless" assault tactics.|
|**Loyal Civil Guards:** Technically proficient but viewed with "instinctive suspicion" by the working class; their loyalty was often contingent on local success.|**The Regulares:** Riffian tribesmen commanded by Spanish officers; experts in stealth, terrain utilization, and psychological warfare.|
|**Trade Union/Party Militias:** High "Ideological motivation" but suffered from a "systemic lack of heavy weapons," maps, and professional command.|**The Army of Africa:** The "spinal column" of the rising; possessed a unified **Africanista mystique** and superior inter-arm coordination.|

### 7. Conclusion: The Legacy of the Republican War Effort

The transition from militias to the People's Army was a friction-filled evolution that ultimately prioritized military centralism over the "revolutionary reality" of 1936.

1. **Structural Incompatibility:** The Republican zone remained a "cauldron of incompatibilities," where the inability to harmonize "state centralism" with "regionalist/libertarian autonomy" prevented the formation of a truly unified political-military strategy.
2. **Institutional Impotence:** The "impotence of the Spanish parliament" (the Cortes) meant that the Republic was forced to rely on executive decrees and political commissars to enforce a discipline that the Nationalists achieved through a cohesive, authoritarian military tradition.
3. **The Victory of Pragmatism over Ideology:** The shift toward professionalization empowered the Communist Party as the only force capable of providing "scientific" organization, but this process alienated the libertarian base, leading to a "civil war within the civil war" that further eroded the Republic's structural integrity.# Report on Republican Conscription and the Formation of the People's Army
