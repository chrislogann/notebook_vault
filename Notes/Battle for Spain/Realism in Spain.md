- **Geopolitical Realpolitik and British Non-Intervention:** The British Foreign Office acted with cold-blooded realism, secretly prepared to sacrifice the Spanish Republic to preserve Mediterranean trade routes and secure commercial relations with Axis powers. They ultimately **preferred a stable, neutral Spain under General Francisco Franco to an unstable Republic**, deliberately turning a blind eye to Italian and German aircraft flying over Gibraltar to supply the Nationalists.
- **Transnational Corporate Support of Fascism:** While democratic governments enforced a strict policy of non-intervention, major American and British corporations engaged in materialist, pragmatical support of Franco’s forces. Companies like **Texaco, Standard Oil of New Jersey, Ford, Studebaker, General Motors, and DuPont actively supplied Franco with essential credits, military trucks, fuel, and bombs**. Franco's foreign ministry later admitted that without American petroleum, trucks, and credit, the Nationalists could never have won.
- **Stalin’s Popular Front Realpolitik:** Soviet foreign policy in Spain was dictated by Stalin's desire to avoid a premature clash with Nazi Germany while attempting to build a rapprochement with Britain and France. The Spanish Communist Party was instructed to follow the Comintern line of **actively concealing the ongoing social revolution and vigorously defending private property and business interests** to keep the Republic looking like a liberal, property-owning democracy to Western observers.
- **The Post-Victory Elimination of Leftist Allies:** Soviet military advisers and Comintern representatives realistically treated their alliance with other Spanish leftist groups as purely tactical. As early as September 1936, General Vladimir Goriev reported to Moscow that **a severe and inevitable struggle to "clean up" and liquidate the anarchists and Trotskyists would be carried out once the Nationalist "whites" were defeated**.
- **Franco’s Calculated Delays to Secure Leadership:** During the Nationalist advance on Madrid in 1936, General Franco realistically delayed his march to relieve the besieged defenders of the Alcázar of Toledo. Wily and politically clear-sighted, **Franco calculated that becoming the "saviour of the Alcázar" would make his political leadership of the Nationalist movement unchallengeable**, whereas a direct assault on Madrid was too high of a military risk before his personal authority was consolidated.
