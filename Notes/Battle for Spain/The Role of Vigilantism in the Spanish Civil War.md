[[9780143037651-The battle for Spain - the Spanish Civil War, 1936-1939 Antony Beevor EPUB_extract]]

Vigilantism and the breakdown of law and order were defining features of the period leading up to and during the Spanish Civil War. The extrajudicial violence began as a deadly cycle of tit-for-tat retaliations before the conflict officially started. **The most significant catalyst was the vigilante assassination of the prominent right-wing leader José Calvo Sotelo**. After Falangist gunmen murdered the left-wing Assault Guard officer Lieutenant José Castillo, Castillo’s comrades completely bypassed legal frameworks to exact immediate revenge. They abducted Calvo Sotelo from his home, shot him in an automobile, and dumped his body in a cemetery. **Nationalists immediately framed this extrajudicial killing as the "final straw," providing massive propaganda value and driving wider popular support for their pre-planned military rebellion**.

Once the civil war commenced, Nationalist rear areas underwent a methodical and ruthless purge of left-wingers, union members, liberals, and Freemasons. **Vigilantes, particularly groups of young, upper-class men known as _señoritos_, formed mobile execution squads using their parents' touring cars in a practice described as "sanctified gangsterism"**. They routinely abducted suspects at night, shot them in the glare of car headlights, and left their bodies in the open as warnings, sometimes pinning union membership cards to their chests as proof of "guilt". The most internationally renowned victim of this Nationalist vigilantism was the poet and playwright Federico García Lorca. Targeted for his liberal sympathies, Lorca was abducted by a death squad, secretly executed without any formal legal process, and left in an unmarked grave. **The vigilantes were so emboldened that one killer explicitly bragged about the murder, stating he shot Lorca because he was a homosexual**.

Rampant vigilantism also consumed the Republican side, where the collapse of state authority triggered a wave of extrajudicial killings known as the "Red Terror". **Revolutionary tribunals called _checas_, anarchist factions, and even opportunistic criminals abducted victims and "took them for a ride"—a cinematic euphemism for secret execution**. Republican vigilantism resulted in an estimated 38,000 deaths, with mass killings heavily concentrated in Madrid and Catalonia. Notable atrocities included the Model Prison Massacre, where enraged militiamen dragged out and shot thirty prominent right-wing prisoners, and the Paracuellos Massacres, where communist security forces executed approximately 2,000 prisoners to prevent them from being freed by advancing Nationalist forces.

This rampant extrajudicial slaughter had profound political and social consequences. The Basque philosopher Miguel de Unamuno initially supported the Nationalist uprising but became deeply horrified by the vigilantism that claimed the lives of close friends, including Lorca and the mayor of Salamanca. **Unamuno courageously delivered a public rebuke against the "necrophilous and senseless" glorification of death at the University of Salamanca, nearly becoming a victim of vigilantism himself when an enraged fascist crowd threatened to lynch him on the spot**. He was saved only by the presence of General Franco's wife on the stage. Ultimately, the unchecked vigilantism that killed Lorca inadvertently protected Unamuno; the massive international outcry over Lorca's murder severely damaged the Nationalist regime's reputation, dissuading General Franco from ordering an official execution for Unamuno's public defiance.
