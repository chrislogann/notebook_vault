**1. Historical Genesis and the Proletarian Roots of the Falange**

The Falange was founded in 1933 by José Antonio Primo de Rivera as a small, fascist-style party. However, its ideological trajectory shifted significantly in 1934 following its merger with the _Juntas de Ofensiva Nacional-Sindicalista_ (JONS). This union introduced a distinctly proletarian and revolutionary element, creating a permanent internal tension between "modern reactionaries"—who prioritized the nationalistic myths of "Old Spain"—and a radicalized "socialist wing." This latter group, the "Old Shirts," viewed themselves as a revolutionary vanguard, often utilizing a rhetoric that mirrored the very Republic they sought to destroy.

Key ideological tenets of this "socialist wing" included:

- **A virulent anti-capitalist stance** that rejected the "suicidal egotism" of the traditional ruling class.
- **A deep-seated resentment toward "upper-class** _**señoritos**_**,"** the decadent elites who prioritized their own property rights over national regeneration.
- **The pursuit of a "National-Sindicalist" revolution**, which sought to replace the existing social order with a state-driven economic system.
- **A commitment to social reform** that, in a striking parallel to the convictions of the Left, suggested that a "coercive redistribution of wealth" was the only path to achieving national unity and universal happiness.

**2. The Nationalist Alliance: A Coalition of Incompatibilities**

While the Nationalist zone appeared cohesive to outside observers, it was in reality a "cauldron of incompatibilities." The radical, anti-capitalist aspirations of the 'Old Shirts' stood in stark contrast to the traditionalist, monarchist, and agrarian interests that formed the backbone of the rising.

**3. The Conflict of Class and Interest: 'Old Shirts' vs. Conservative Elites**

The Nationalist zone was defined by a "Fatal Paradox." While the movement was united by the three cohesive extremes of centralism, authoritarianism, and right-wing politics, it was internally fractured by fundamentally different social visions.

### Class

The primary axis of conflict pitted the Falangist desire for social revolution against the entrenched interests of the landed elite. The 'Old Shirts' viewed the "suicidal egotism" of the landowners as a barrier to national salvation, whereas the CEDA and Monarchist factions viewed the Falange’s talk of wealth redistribution as a "bolshevik" threat dressed in Nationalist colors.

### Authoritarianism vs. Liberty

Though all factions rejected liberal democracy, their definitions of order differed. The Falange practiced "revolutionary gymnastics"—a fascist-style vanguardism based on mass mobilization. This conflicted with the military’s traditional, institutional authoritarianism. The generals viewed the army as the "spinal column" of the state and regarded the Falange's street-fighting radicalism as a source of potential instability.

### Centralism vs. Regionalism

While united in their visceral hatred of Basque and Catalan separatism, the "new state" envisioned by the Falange was a modernizing, totalitarian construct. This clashed with the Carlists’ vision, which, despite its centralism in 1936, remained rooted in a populist, traditionalist past that rejected the secularizing and modernizing tendencies of European-style fascism.

**4. The Dilution of Radicalism: The Influx of 1936–1937**

The original militants—the 'Old Shirts'—quickly found their influence marginalized following the July 1936 rising. As the party became the sole legal vehicle for political participation in the Nationalist zone, it was swamped by those seeking safety or opportunism.

"The ‘leftist’ faction was even more disadvantaged by the vast influx of opportunists in 1936 and 1937. Its influence was crushed when Franco institutionalized the movement... [by] modern reactionaries who prioritized Old Spain over social reform."

This influx shifted the party's center of gravity away from its proletarian, anti-capitalist roots and toward a nationalistic, Catholic orthodoxy. The "Old Shirts" were effectively buried under a mass of "modern reactionaries" who cared more for the preservation of property than for social syndicalism.

**5. Franco’s Strategic Neutralization of Manuel Hedilla**

Following the death of José Antonio Primo de Rivera, General Francisco Franco moved with cold precision to assume absolute control over the Falangist movement. He recognized that for the "new state" to survive, the Falange had to be broken as an independent political force and converted into a servant of the military.

**Steps to Political Dominance:**

1. **Exploitation of the Leadership Vacuum:** Franco utilized the absence of the "Absent One" (José Antonio) to present himself as the only figure capable of uniting the fragmented Nationalist factions.
2. **Coercive Military Superiority:** Franco leveraged his command of the Army of Africa—the elite legionnaires and Moroccan _regulares_—as a shadow over the party leadership, ensuring that any dissent from the "socialist wing" was met with the threat of overwhelming force.
3. **Paramilitary Dominance:** By ensuring the loyalty of the Civil Guard and the state’s security apparatus, Franco stripped the Falange of its ability to act as a "revolutionary vanguard" independent of the state.
4. **Neutralization of Rivals:** Manuel Hedilla, the leader in Corunna and the representative of the 'Old Shirt' faction, was strategically sidelined and eventually suppressed, ensuring the party’s complete submission to the military’s "spinal column."

**6. The Decree of Unification and the Birth of FET y de las JONS**

In April 1937, Franco institutionalized the movement through the Decree of Unification. He forcibly amalgamated the radical Falange and the traditionalist Carlists into a single entity: the _Falange Española Tradicionalista y de las JONS_ (FET). By appointing himself as Jefe (Chief), Franco effectively decapitated the original movement's political autonomy.

The symbolic visual changes resulting from this union reflected this "cauldron of incompatibilities" being fused by decree:

- **The Blue Shirt:** Retained from the original Falange uniform to signify the "social" element.
- **The Red Beret:** Adopted from the Carlist _requetés_ to signify the traditionalist and Catholic foundation.

**7. Transformation: From Revolutionary Vanguard to Bureaucratic Tool**

The "metamorphosis" of the Falange from 1936 to its final institutionalized form represented the total victory of the traditional establishment over the radical fascist dream. The "Old Shirts" were not the architects of the New Spain, but merely the foot soldiers of a traditionalist reaction.

- **Takeaway 1:** The Falange was transformed from a fascist-style revolutionary movement into a vast bureaucratic tool for social control, stripped of its proletarian radicalism.
- **Takeaway 2:** The "socialist" rhetoric of the early Falange was successfully neutralized by Franco, who converted the party into a mechanism to mobilize the masses in support of a traditionalist, military-led state.
- **Takeaway 3:** By 1937, the movement had become the servant of the "trinity" of the Spanish establishment—the Army, the Church, and the Monarchy—effectively ending the 'Old Shirts'’ hope for a social revolution.
