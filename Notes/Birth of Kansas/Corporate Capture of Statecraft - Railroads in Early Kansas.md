In the mid-19th century, the boundary between corporate enterprises and state politics in Kansas was virtually non-existent, as "politicians and corporate interests were frequently the same people". Railroad corporations and their promoters exerted a massive, corrupting control over early statecraft:

- **Politicians as Corporate Promoters:** High-ranking state and federal officials were simultaneously directors and active promoters of the very companies they regulated. For instance, Governor Charles Robinson and State Senator R. S. Stevens served as directors of the Leavenworth, Pawnee, and Western Railroad Company (L., P., & W.), while Senator S. C. Pomeroy and C. K. Holliday were the chief promoters of the Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railroad.
- **The Exploitation of Indian Treaties:** Before Congress enacted direct federal land grants, railroad companies secured indirect aid by manipulating treaties with Native American tribes. The L., P., & W. acquired hundreds of thousands of acres of Delaware and Pottawatomie lands for the incredibly low price of $1.25 per acre. The politicians who facilitated these treaties were handsomely compensated; individuals like Thomas Ewing Jr., J. C. Stone, and A. J. Isacks received tens of thousands of acres, while Senator Pomeroy reportedly received 17 sections of land simply for dropping his political opposition to the transaction.
- **Diverting Public Assets:** The political influence of the railroads was strong enough to override the state's constitution. In 1841, the Federal Government allotted 500,000 acres of land to Kansas, which the state constitution explicitly set aside to fund common schools. However, in 1866, the legislature successfully diverted this entire 500,000-acre tract away from the schools and gave it directly to four railroad companies.
- **Electoral Corruption and "Famine Politics":** Political campaigns and Senate elections were heavily dictated by competing railroad factions seeking federal subsidies. During the devastating Kansas famine of 1860–1861, S. C. Pomeroy leveraged his position as a relief distributor to "ride into the United States Senate on the famine horse", using relief funds and goods to buy the votes of state legislators.
- **Constitutional Safeguards:** Despite this corporate dominance, the framers of the Wyandotte Constitution successfully implemented one major financial safeguard. Remembering how other states, such as Indiana, had been driven into ruinous debt by unregulated speculators and public work schemes, they explicitly prohibited the state of Kansas from directly carrying on works of public improvement, thereby protecting the government itself from becoming liable for corporate construction debts.
