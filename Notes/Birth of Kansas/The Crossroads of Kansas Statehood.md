The debate over Kansas statehood was fiercely contested, with the arguments dividing largely along party lines between the Democratic Party (opposing immediate admission) and the Republican/Free-State Party (favoring it).

**The Position Against Statehood** The Democratic Party and some conservative Free-State papers, such as the _Herald of Freedom_, opposed immediate statehood primarily on economic grounds.

- **Heavy Taxation:** Opponents warned that assuming a State government would impose a crushing burden of taxation on the people, who were already struggling, injuring the territory for the next twenty years.
- **Loss of Federal Aid:** Democratic papers like the _Western Argus_ argued it was better to remain a territory until the Federal government provided land grants, erected public buildings, and built bridges, rather than being "over anxious to pay for them ourselves".
- **Political Ambition:** Opponents claimed that the push for statehood was not for the good of the territory, but was driven solely by politicians who "hoped for office under the State government".
- **National Political Strategy & Legal Objections:** In Washington, opponents of admission argued that Kansas lacked the necessary population (claiming it had only 69,700 instead of the required 93,000) and that its proposed boundaries illegally included Cherokee Indian lands. More strategically, national Democrats were unwilling to admit another Republican state just before the 1860 Presidential election and attempted to delay admission to force Kansas to annex the Democratic-leaning Platte River region.

**The Position For Statehood** The great majority of the Republican (Free-State) Party actively favored immediate admission to the Union and aggressively countered the Democrats' arguments.

- **Failure of Federal Support:** Republicans retorted that the promise of Federal aid as a territory was an illusion. The _Lawrence Republican_ pointed out that Kansas had been a territory for about five years under a Democratic federal administration and yet had obtained "no railroads, no bridges, no public buildings" from them.
- **Territorial Corruption:** Republicans argued that they needed statehood to escape the corrupt territorial administration, charging that Democratic officials had "squandered and pocketed some $75,000 of the people's money at Lecompton".
- **Political Fairness:** Republicans pointed to a double standard in Congress regarding population. They questioned why a territory like Oregon, which had only eight or nine thousand voters, was eagerly rushed into the Union, while Kansas, which had nearly double that number of voters, was being excluded.
