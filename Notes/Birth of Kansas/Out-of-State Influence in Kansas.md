Out-of-state actors played a pivotal role in the early political, military, and economic development of Kansas. Just as foreign powers used Spain as a battleground, external forces—ranging from Missouri border ruffians to Eastern politicians and financiers—used Kansas as a proxy to fight national battles over slavery, political dominance, and economic exploitation.

**Border Warfare and Southern Secessionists** The proximity of Missouri, a slave state, led to constant cross-border violence. **"Border Ruffians"** from Missouri, such as Sheriff Jones, clashed repeatedly with Free-State "Jayhawkers" over land claims and the enforcement of the Fugitive Slave Law. When the Civil War began, this guerrilla warfare escalated. Missouri's Governor C. F. Jackson virtually declared war on the U.S. government, prompting Kansas Governor Charles Robinson to prepare the state for invasion. Confederate forces, including those under General Price, threatened to "wipe Kansas off the map," while notorious raiders like Quantrill sacked Kansas towns, including Olathe, Shawnee, and Lawrence, murdering citizens and looting property. Furthermore, the Confederate government actively encouraged Indian tribes (such as the Cherokees, Creeks, and Seminoles) to attack Kansas settlers with the promise of restoring their lands.

**Eastern Political and Financial Influence** Eastern politicians and abolitionists heavily influenced the formation of the Kansas Republican Party and its local elections.

- **National Political Figures:** Prominent national leaders took a direct interest in Kansas politics. Horace Greeley personally attended the 1859 Osawatomie convention to help draft the local Republican party platform, and Abraham Lincoln directly advised Kansas leaders like Mark W. Delahay on maintaining the party's anti-slavery objectives rather than lowering their standards just to gather recruits.
- **Campaign Financing:** Eastern money was funneled into Kansas to control elections. Congressional candidate M. F. Conway explicitly requested campaign funds from Eastern backers like G. L. Stearns of Boston, and received a $1,000 draft from Governor Morgan of New York, which was used to pay off partisan tools and control local nominations.
- **Politicized Famine Relief:** Even humanitarian aid was leveraged for political gain. Famine relief organized by Thaddeus Hyatt of New York and the New England Kansas Relief Committee (directed by Thomas H. Webb of Boston) was distributed disproportionately to Republican strongholds. This heavily financed, out-of-state charity operation was instrumental in ensuring S. C. Pomeroy's election to the U.S. Senate.

**Federal Military Patronage and Interference** Federal authorities in Washington D.C. fundamentally disrupted the internal governance of Kansas by intervening in its military organization. Senator James H. Lane frequently bypassed Governor Robinson by securing direct authorization from Secretary of War Edwin M. Stanton and President Lincoln to recruit regiments, appoint officers, and even enlist African American and Indian troops in direct defiance of the Governor's authority. This out-of-state military interference paralyzed the state's ability to defend itself, as Lane and the War Department withheld military resources and funds from Robinson, leaving border settlers exposed to guerrilla raids while Lane built a personal military machine.

**Economic Exploitation by Out-of-State Speculators** Much like the economic exploitation you noted in Spain, external speculators profited heavily off the financially desperate new state. When Kansas attempted to sell state bonds to fund its nascent government, out-of-state agents like Robert G. Corwin of Ohio collaborated with Federal officials, including Secretary of the Interior Caleb B. Smith. Through a deeply corrupt scheme, these speculators forced the state of Kansas to sell its bonds at 60 cents on the dollar, while secretly negotiating to sell those same bonds to the Federal government for Indian trust funds at 85 cents on the dollar, pocketing the massive difference at the expense of the state.
