[[Birth of Kansas]]

While the sources do not explicitly use the phrase "corporate takeover," they provide overwhelming evidence that **railroad corporations and their promoters exerted a massive, often corrupting, control over early Kansas politics, elections, and land distribution.** Politicians and corporate interests were frequently the same people, and railroad agendas dictated many of the new state's earliest decisions.

Here is how railway interests dominated early Kansas statecraft:

- **Controlling Elections and Politicians:** The fate of political candidates was heavily tied to railroad companies. Even during the territorial period, railroad companies fought over the election of delegates. Later, the election of Kansas's first United States Senators was largely decided by railroad interests, as candidates like S. C. Pomeroy and James H. Lane were backed by rival railroad factions seeking federal land grants.
- **Politicians as Corporate Promoters:** State and federal officials were intimately involved in the corporations themselves. Governor Charles Robinson and State Senator R. S. Stevens were directors of the Leavenworth, Pawnee, and Western Railroad Company (L., P., & W. R. R. Co.). Senator S. C. Pomeroy and C. K. Holliday were main promoters of the Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railroad.
- **Profiting from Indian Treaties:** Before Congress passed direct federal land grants for railroads in 1862, indirect federal aid was secured through treaties with Native American tribes. The L., P., & W. R. R. Co. acquired hundreds of thousands of acres of Delaware and Pottawatomie Indian lands at the low price of $1.25 an acre. The politicians and lobbyists who negotiated these treaties were richly rewarded; individuals like Thomas Ewing Jr., J. C. Stone, and A. J. Isacks received tens of thousands of acres, and Senator Pomeroy reportedly received seventeen sections of land simply for remaining "neutral" and dropping his opposition to the transaction.
- **Diverting Public School Land:** In 1841, the Federal Government allotted 500,000 acres of land to the state, which the Kansas constitution explicitly set aside for the support of common schools. However, the influence of the railroads was so strong that in 1866, the legislature diverted these 500,000 acres away from the schools and gave them entirely to four railroad companies.
- **Locating the State Capital:** The fierce battle to locate the state capital at Topeka was heavily backed by the Leavenworth, Pawnee, and Western Railroad interests, who traded political representation (by gerrymandering convention delegates) to secure votes for Topeka.

**Safeguards Against Complete Financial Ruin** Despite this immense corporate influence over the politicians and the land, the state did manage to protect itself from direct financial bankruptcy. Remembering the disastrous experiences of other states like Indiana—where state governments became heavily indebted due to unregulated speculators and internal improvement schemes—the framers of the Wyandotte Constitution explicitly prohibited the state from becoming a party to carrying on works of public improvement.

Therefore, while railroad corporations successfully "took over" the politicians, the elections, and massive tracts of public and Indian lands, the state's constitution protected the government itself from becoming directly financially liable for the corporations' construction debts.
