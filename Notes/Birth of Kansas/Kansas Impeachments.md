[[Birth of Kansas]]

While the term "political purge" is not explicitly used in the historical records of early Kansas, the political landscape between 1859 and 1863 was defined by intense factionalism and exclusionary tactics that functioned similarly to purges. Based on the provided notes, the role of these dynamics can be analyzed through the following lenses:

### 1. The Use of Impeachment as a Political Weapon
The most direct evidence of a "purge" occurred during the 1862 impeachment trials. Following the "Bond Swindle"—a scandal involving the sale of state bonds to railroad promoter R. S. Stevens—the legislature moved against the administration of Governor Charles Robinson.
- **Targeted Removal:** Secretary of State J. W. Robinson and Auditor George S. Hillyer were convicted and removed from office. 
- **Partisan Consolidation:** The legislature was noted for "packing the State Senate" specifically to facilitate these partisan impeachment trials. This served as a mechanism to neutralize the Robinson faction and consolidate power within the opposing camp, effectively purging the executive branch of the Governor's allies.

### 2. Factional Displacement and the "Spoils of Office"
The rivalry between the "Robinson faction" (conservative/legalistic) and the "Lane faction" (radical/opportunistic) created a cycle of displacement.
- **Military Patronage:** The feud between Governor Robinson and General James H. Lane over military commissions functioned as a constant struggle for control. Lane used his influence in Washington to bypass the Governor’s authority, effectively purging the Governor's influence from the state's military organization.
- **Legislative Paralysis:** The obsession with the "spoils of office" and the election of U.S. Senators meant that political energy was directed toward "caucusing" and "combinations and ruptures." This environment made it nearly impossible for those outside of the dominant factions to participate in meaningful governance, as the state's business was entirely consumed by these internal power struggles.

### 3. Corporate-Driven Exclusion
The influence of railroad corporations acted as a systemic filter that purged or sidelined politicians who did not align with corporate agendas.
- **Railroad Dominance:** Because politicians and corporate promoters were often the same people (e.g., Governor Robinson and Senator S. C. Pomeroy), the political system was effectively "purged" of interests that conflicted with railroad expansion. 
- **Gerrymandering:** To secure the state capital at Topeka, railroad interests manipulated the political process by gerrymandering convention delegates. This effectively disenfranchised or "purged" the voting power of regions or individuals who opposed the railroad-backed location of the capital.

### 4. The Failure of the "Fresh Start"
The Wyandotte Convention attempted to "purge" the state of professional politicians by electing "common farmers" who were intended to be free of personal ambition. However, this attempt at a clean slate was quickly overwhelmed by the existing political culture. The rapid transformation of these farmers into "Captains, Colonels, Generals, and Governors" suggests that the systemic pressures of the era—specifically the pursuit of federal patronage and corporate land grants—acted as a force that purged the government of its original reformist, idealistic intent.

**Summary**
In early Kansas, political purges were not necessarily formal state-sanctioned removals of ideological enemies, but rather **tactical maneuvers within a high-stakes spoils system.** Through impeachment, the manipulation of military appointments, and the consolidation of power via railroad-backed factions, political leaders systematically neutralized their rivals to secure control over land, federal aid, and state offices.
