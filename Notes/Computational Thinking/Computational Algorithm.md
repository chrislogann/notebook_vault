**An algorithm is a plan consisting of a set of step-by-step instructions designed to resolve a specific problem.** As one of the four cornerstones of Computer Science, algorithms are essential because computers cannot solve problems without being given precise instructions on exactly what to do and how to do it. If you have ever tied your shoelaces, made a cup of tea, got dressed, or prepared a meal, you have already followed an algorithm in your everyday life.

### **The Planning and Decomposition Process**

Before writing any code or computer program, a solution must be thoroughly planned to ensure it is correct. This planning process relies on other core computational thinking techniques:

- **Decomposition:** You use decomposition to break a complex problem down into smaller, more manageable parts.
- **Reassembling the Pieces:** Once the problem is broken down, you plan how those smaller parts fit back together in a suitable, logical order to solve the overall problem.
- **Defining the Flow:** This finalized, orderly sequence of actions is represented as an algorithm.

### **Core Characteristics of an Algorithm**

For an algorithm to be successful and easily understood by both humans and computers, it must be highly structured and contain three fundamental elements:

1. **A Clear Starting Point:** A defined beginning of the process.
2. **Clear Instructions in Between:** Each individual instruction must be a single, unambiguous action that can be performed by a computer processor.
3. **A Clear Finishing Point:** A defined endpoint where the problem is successfully resolved.

### **How Algorithms are Represented**

Algorithms act as the starting point for creating any computer program. Developers typically plan and represent these algorithms in two ways before writing the actual code:

- **Flowcharts (Flow Diagrams):** A visual diagram showing the step-by-step process, utilizing standardized boxes to represent individual steps, decisions, inputs, and outputs.
- **Pseudocode:** A planning method where instructions are written out in plain English rather than a programming language, allowing developers to map out the program's logic easily before coding.

### **The "Garbage In, Garbage Out" Rule**

A computer is entirely reliant on the instructions it is given. It cannot think for itself, meaning **computers are only as good as the algorithms they are provided.** If you provide a computer with a poorly designed or incorrect algorithm, it will inevitably produce a poor or incorrect result—a fundamental concept in computer science known as **"Garbage in, garbage out".**

Once a high-quality algorithm is successfully designed, it can be programmed to automate a vast range of tasks, including **calculations, data processing, and automation.**
