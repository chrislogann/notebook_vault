**Abstraction is one of the four cornerstones of Computer Science that involves filtering out—essentially ignoring—the characteristics and specific details we do not need in order to concentrate on those we do.** In computational thinking, this process occurs after we decompose a complex problem into smaller parts and look for patterns among and within those smaller problems. By filtering out irrelevant details, we can focus strictly on what is necessary to create a representation, or general idea, of what we are trying to solve.

### **Distinguishing General Characteristics from Specific Details**

To abstract effectively, you must learn to separate universal traits from individual variations.

- **General Characteristics:** These are the common elements shared by all instances of a pattern. For example, in a pattern involving cats, all cats share the general characteristics of having eyes, a tail, fur, a liking for fish, and the ability to make meowing sounds.
- **Specific Details (Specifics):** These are non-essential, individual variations, such as black fur, a long tail, green eyes, a love of salmon, or a loud meow.

### **The Mechanics of Filtering**

During abstraction, you evaluate both the general characteristics and the specifics to determine what is actually relevant to solving your immediate problem.

- **Filtering Irrelevant Characteristics:** If your goal is simply to draw a basic cat, you do not need to know what sound a cat makes or that it likes to eat fish. Because these behavioral characteristics are irrelevant to the visual task, they are filtered out.
- **Filtering Specifics:** While you do need to know that a cat has eyes, a tail, and fur to draw it, you do not need to know the specific size or color of these features. These specific variations are filtered out, leaving you with a basic, universal concept of what a cat looks like so you can describe how to draw one.

### **The Goal of Abstraction: Creating a "Model"**

The ultimate output of the abstraction process is a **model**—a general idea of what the problem is and how to solve it. A model is formed by actively removing all specific details and discarding any identified patterns that will not help you solve your problem.

### **Why Abstraction is Vital to Problem Solving**

Failing to abstract can easily lead you to **design the wrong solution** to a problem. Without abstraction, you risk mistaking a specific variation for a universal rule. For instance, without abstracting, you might analyze a single cat and incorrectly conclude that _all_ cats have long tails and short fur. By applying abstraction, you successfully separate core requirements (cats have tails and fur) from non-essential specifics (not all tails are long, and not all fur is short). This ensures you form a clearer, highly accurate model of the problem.
