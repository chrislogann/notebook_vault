[[9780375424588-Twelve Days - The Story of the 1956 Hungarian Revolution Victor Sebestyen, Victor Sebestyen PDF_extract]]

During the 1956 Hungarian Revolution, vigilantism emerged as a defining and terrifying feature of the street-level uprising. Driven by years of brutal oppression under a Soviet-backed regime, Hungarian citizens formed **spontaneous lynch mobs to deliver "instant justice" against the widely despised secret police, the State Security Authority (AVO)**. Because the AVO was viewed as the chief instrument of state terror, crowds frequently bypassed formal law to dispense gruesome "popular judgments" directly on the streets.

**The Catalyst: Atrocities Against Civilians** Mob violence rarely occurred in a vacuum; it was most often triggered by specific, horrific atrocities committed by the AVO against unarmed protesters. When secret police officers opened fire on civilians, the surviving crowds frequently retaliated with overwhelming force.

A stark example occurred in the industrial town of Miskolc. After AVO Captain Laszlo Csurke ordered his men to fire into a crowd protesting the arrest of young rebels, the enraged demonstrators returned with armed miners. When the local military garrison arrived and sided with the rebels, the mob besieged the police headquarters, eventually ransacking the building and massacring Csurke along with another AVO officer.

**Gruesome Retribution: Republic Square and Mosonmagyaróvár** The scale of the mob violence was staggering. During the siege of the Communist Party headquarters at Republic Square in Budapest, insurgents besieged the building after AVO officers fired on protesters. The aftermath was utterly brutal: **twenty-three AVO officers were killed as they surrendered, with many shot point-blank, beaten, or kicked to death**. Victims were strung up by their feet, and the unit's commander, Colonel Jozsef Papp, was hung with cable wire, doused in petrol, and burned.

The vigilantism in Mosonmagyaróvár was equally devastating. Following a peaceful demonstration where AVO Lieutenant Jozsef Stefko ordered machine guns and hand grenades deployed against the crowd—killing 52 people and injuring 86—the enraged survivors laid siege to the local AVO building. **The mob captured two AVO men, beat them into "bloody objects," and literally tore them into pieces "as if this had been the work of wild animals"**. The vigilante justice concluded the next morning when a massive crowd stormed the hospital where Lieutenant Stefko was recovering from surgery. The vengeful crowd dragged him from his stretcher, violently trampled him to death, and hung his body upside down by the ankles from a tree on Lenin Street.

**The Chaos and the Code of "Blood Money"** Because this street-level justice was instantaneous and chaotic, it was highly prone to deadly errors. Innocent people were sometimes targeted due to mistaken identity. In one harrowing instance, a man named Kelemen was in the process of being hanged before someone recognized him and confirmed he was not in the AVO. Furthermore, regular military and air force personnel were occasionally attacked by fanatics simply because their blue uniform collars resembled those of the secret police.

Despite the frenzied brutality, the mobs adhered to a specific, symbolic code of conduct. **Victims were frequently left hanging by their legs from trees or lampposts, accompanied by a uniquely Hungarian gesture: crowds would deliberately leave piles of the victim's cash entirely untouched on the ground beneath them**. This signaled that the money was tainted "blood money" and that no honest citizen would stoop to thievery.

**The Aftermath and Condemnation** In total, it is estimated that between 90 and 100 AVO officers were lynched during the uprising. **The leaders of the revolution—including associates of Prime Minister Imre Nagy—strongly condemned this vigilante violence, fearing it would defile the purity of their cause and highlight a dangerous lack of control on the streets**. They urged the public to halt the lynchings and insisted that the AVO should face formal trials in courts of law rather than the passion of the mob. Nonetheless, the pent-up fury of the population could not be easily quelled, making these lynch mobs an indelible and terrifying chapter of the Hungarian Revolution.
